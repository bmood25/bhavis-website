Sakshi

"Write what is true for you right now."

A journaling app built on a simple constraint: it never tells you what your writing means. Most AI journaling tools respond with encouragement, advice, or an interpretation of your mood, but Sakshi does none of that. It reads back only patterns already present in your own words, quoted exactly as written, with no persona, no opinions, and no first-person voice of its own. The design principle is drawn from Advaita Vedanta's practice of neti neti ("not this, not this"): clarity through subtraction rather than addition. If a reflection doesn't emerge from something you actually wrote, nothing is shown. Built the full pipeline myself: an input-classification layer that distinguishes personal reflection from assigned writing or third-party text, and a verification layer that checks every quoted excerpt against the original entry to guarantee nothing shown back was smoothed, paraphrased, or fabricated.

  • Stack: Next.js, TypeScript, Supabase, Anthropic API
  • Role: Sole builder / designer

Stencil

"Have we drifted from what we said we stood for?"

An organization-wide alignment tool that compares what a company declares it values against what its people actually experience. Leaders define the org's stated values and tie ongoing projects to them; employees privately reflect on real moments (decisions, tensions, wins) without ever exposing who said what. Once enough independent voices agree, a pattern surfaces, and Stencil flags where a project's declared purpose and its lived reality genuinely diverge, or confirms where they align. Built around a structural privacy guarantee, not just a policy promise: no raw reflection is ever readable by anyone but its author, enforced at the database layer.

  • Stack: Next.js, TypeScript, Supabase (Postgres + Row-Level Security)
  • Role: Sole builder / designer

Vichara

"What you keep returning to is trying to tell you something."

A reflective reading journal for scripture, poetry, or any text that stops you mid-page. Most bookmarking tools treat each saved passage as an isolated moment: you save it, and it just sits there. Vichara does the opposite: every entry gets a suggested theme, editable and entirely yours to keep, rename, or reject, and over time those entries cluster together so patterns in what you're drawn to become visible rather than buried in a list. It's built to work across any tradition, or none, whether it's scripture, a line of poetry, or a quote that caught you off guard: the app doesn't assume what kind of text matters to you.

  • Stack: Expo (React Native), TypeScript, Firebase, Claude API
  • Role: Sole builder / designer

Status: In development

Philosophy & Interests

I believe that innovation is strongest when it’s rooted in a core philosophy. For me, that philosophy is Advaita Vedanta, the nondual school of Hindu thought systematized by Adi Shankaracharya in the 8th century CE. Its central claim is simple to state, but hard to fully grasp: the sense that you are a separate self, cut off from everything else, is not the deepest truth. Beneath that sense of separateness is one undivided consciousness (what Advaita calls Brahman) and everything, including you, is an expression of it.

In more practical terms, it holds that each of us carries an immense, often unrealized strength and courage within. I believe that humane strength should sit at the center of any product. The product itself is simply a means through which we come to realize ourselves.

“Truth, and nothing but truth, is the watchword of the Advaitist.” — Swami Vivekananda
